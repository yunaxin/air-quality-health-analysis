## Project Overview
Our main area of interest for this project is to find a way to demonstrate the use of statistics and data analytics within the health/environment domains as they impact the lives of many people. After considering various areas that we can explore for this project, we have decided to focus on health problems that might arise due to issues in air quality, with the goal of identifying statistical relationships between air quality indicators and health outcomes. 
## Data Used for Project
The data needed to explore the topic mentioned above will consist of two main parts. The first part would be to gather air quality data which is important for tracking pollution levels that can impact human health. We can acquire this data from the EPA’s Air Quality System which provides air quality data measured from monitors throughout the United States. There are various types of air quality accessible through the EPA and some types of data we would focus on are air quality index and pollutant levels such as PM2.5, PM10, Ozone, Nitrogen Dioxide, Sulfur Dioxide, and Carbon Monoxide. The second part would be to gather health outcome data to see the underlying causes of death. We can acquire this data from CDC Wonder which provides underlying cause of death data measured across counties throughout the United States. For our topic of focus, we can focus on deaths related to respiratory issues as it is related to air quality. Merging the two datasets will be done by looking at the data from both datasets on a year/county level from 2018-2025. We anticipate some challenges processing all the data, but this large quantity of data can help improve the quality of our analysis. 
## Proposed Dataset Analyses
Before we begin any meaningful data analysis, we will first ensure that we have properly cleaned the data. This process includes but is not limited to addressing missing values, normalized naming schemes, and performing necessary aggregations to ensure data consistency and accuracy. Additionally, we will include a section explaining the variable names and definitions, units of measurement, and assumptions that were made during the preprocessing of the data. 
Once the preprocessing and preliminary data preparation steps have been completed, we will shift our focus towards exploratory data analysis (EDA) where we seek to better understand trends in air pollution and respiratory related deaths between 2018 and 2025. During EDA, we will find summary statistics such as the means, medians, and standard deviations of variables within our data. Additionally, we can determine the correlation between the two variables and possibly determine some other crucial pieces of information, such as how skewed the data is. 
To formally address the relationship between respiratory related mortality rates and air pollution, we would then shift our focus towards hypothesis testing with multiple linear regression. For these tests, respiratory related deaths would be our dependent variable while the pollutant levels would serve as our independent variables. This approach will allow us to evaluate the statistical significance between respiratory related mortality and the effects of the different pollutants on health. We will also clearly state null and alternative hypotheses and access key regression assumptions to ensure the validity of our statistical conclusions.
To strengthen our analysis, we will estimate similar regression models using other similar underlying causes of death (e.g., cardiovascular death). The comparison of air pollution with other forms of mortality allow us to determine whether or not air pollution has a strong and more consistent association with respiratory mortality as opposed to other forms of death.
Finally, to address any issues with confounding variables, we will evaluate whether the observed relationships are vastly different between different groups. Where possible, we will incorporate statistical controls such as county and year fixed effects where appropriate. This will help account for unobserved county-level characteristics and broader time trends that could influence mortality rates.
## Expected Outcomes
Based on prior public health research and existing literature, we expect to observe a statistically significant positive relationship between air pollution levels and respiratory related mortality rates at the country year level.
More specifically, we hypothesize that increase in fine particulate matter and ozone levels will be most strongly associated with higher respiratory related death rates. These pollutants are known to penetrate deeply into the lungs and have been linked to asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and other respiratory conditions. We expect these pollutants to exhibit larger effect sizes compared to pollutants such as carbon monoxide or sulfur dioxide.
In our multiple linear regression models, we anticipate that pollutant levels show positive regression coefficients when predicting respiratory mortality, indicating that higher pollution levels are associated with increased respiratory related deaths. We expect these relationships to remain statistically significant even after incorporating country and year fixed effects thorough effect sizes may decrease once broader time trends and regional characteristics are controlled for. 
When comparing respiratory mortality to other causes of death such as cardiovascular mortality, we expect the association between air pollution and respiratory mortality to be stronger and more consistent. This would support the hypothesis that air pollution disproportionately impacts respiratory health relative to other mortality categories. 
However, we also recognize that the strength of the observed relationships may vary across countries and over time due to differences in healthcare access, demographic composition, socioeconomic factors, and environmental regulations. Therefore, while we expect to observe meaningful associations, we do not assume the population alone fully explains variation in mortality rates. 
Overall we expect our trends to demonstrate that poorer air quality is associated with worse respiratory health outcomes, reinforcing public health importance of environmental monitoring and pollution control policies.
